\section{Overview}
% ==================================================================

The task is to extract numbered equations from quantum physics arXiv
papers and produce, for each equation, a meaning sentence, symbol
definitions, inter-equation relations, and an audit trail.
Because the required dataset must be traceable to the paper, generated
explanations are avoided.

The approach is a hybrid retrieval system inspired by RAG\@.
Instead of dynamic user queries, the project has three fixed query types:
equation meaning, symbol meaning, and equation relation.
The pipeline parses arXiv HTML into equations, text chunks, context
sentences, and MathML symbols, then embeds equations and chunks with
\textbf{MathBERT}~\cite{peng2021mathbert} (\texttt{witiko/mathberta}).
\textbf{MathBERT} is used because it is trained for mathematical and scientific
language, so it can compare equations with explanatory text better than
a general text embedding model.
BM25 and \textbf{MathBERT} provide model-based retrieval, while deterministic
rules choose only source-backed spans.
This keeps the method simple, general across papers, and auditable.

\section{Building Embedding Space}

This section explains how the pipeline prepares the shared retrieval
space used by the later extraction stages.
Stages~2--4 convert each arXiv HTML paper into structured inputs for
embedding:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Stage 2: document parsing}
        reads the cached HTML and stores sections, paragraphs,
        sentences, raw equation blocks, labels, MathML, cross-references,
        and local before/after context in
        \path{data/pipeline/documents/<paper_id>.json}.
  \item \textbf{Stage 3: chunk construction}
        turns the structured document into searchable units, including
        sentence chunks, paragraph chunks, equation-neighbourhood chunks,
        and cross-reference chunks in
        \path{data/pipeline/chunks/<paper_id>.json}.
  \item \textbf{Stage 4: equation extraction}
        selects the numbered display equations for the dataset and
        stores their equation ID, \LaTeX{}, HTML anchor, section,
        document order, and resolved context sentences in
        \path{data/pipeline/equations/<paper_id>.json}.
\end{itemize}

Stage~5 is the key step that turns these structured objects into an
embedding space.

\noindent\textbf{Responsible file:}
\path{Our approach/stage05_build_embeddings.py}\\
\textbf{Output:}
\path{data/pipeline/embeddings/<paper_id>.json}\\
and \path{data/pipeline/embeddings/<paper_id>.npz}.

This stage embeds equations and text chunks with \textbf{MathBERT}.
For each equation it builds equation-only, summary-context, and
sentence-context vectors.
For the paper text it embeds the retrieval chunks from Stage~3.
The vectors are saved in \texttt{.npz}; the JSON file records row
metadata and source text for auditing.

paper_id: 2401.00059
model: witiko/mathberta
normalized: true
dimension: 768
rows:
  vector_id: 2401.00059:equation:1:summary
  vector_kind: summary
  source_text: before text, equation text, after text
  row: 1
\end{CodeSnippet}


\section{Retrieval and Extraction}

\paragraph{Equation meanings.}
After Stage~5, the pipeline uses the same embedding space for all final
annotations.  Equation meanings are selected from candidate sentences
collected by cross-references, cue phrases, BM25 retrieval, and local
proximity.  Candidates are ranked by
\[
\operatorname{score}(c)=0.5\cos(e,c)+0.2\operatorname{BM25}(e,c)
+0.2\operatorname{source}(c)+0.1\operatorname{prox}(e,c)-\operatorname{penalty}(c).
\]
The terms are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\cos(e,c)$: \textbf{MathBERT} similarity between equation
        context and candidate sentence;
  \item $\operatorname{BM25}(e,c)$: lexical overlap with the equation
        query;
  \item $\operatorname{source}(c)$: bonus for reliable evidence such as
        cross-references or cue phrases;
  \item $\operatorname{prox}(e,c)$: preference for text near the
        equation;
  \item $\operatorname{penalty}(c)$: penalty for procedural,
        incomplete, math-heavy, or symbol-definition-like sentences.
\end{itemize}


Stage~6b then applies a rule-based postprocessing step in
\path{stage06_postprocess_meanings.py}.
The rules shorten the top candidate sentence into an extractive
meaningful phrase.

\begin{CodeSnippet}
Before postprocessing:
The covariance matrix of the TMSV state can be obtained by setting
n_{\\text{th}}=0 in Eq. (1).

After postprocessing:
covariance matrix of the TMST state
\end{CodeSnippet}

\paragraph{Symbol definitions.}
Symbols are extracted mainly from MathML because it preserves subscripts,
superscripts, and decorated notation; a \LaTeX{} fallback is used when
MathML is incomplete.  For each symbol, the system builds aliases from
its canonical form, base symbol, Unicode form, and \LaTeX{} spelling.
These aliases are combined with definition phrases such as ``where $x$
is'' or ``$x$ denotes'' and embedded as a query.  The top retrieved
sentences are checked with deterministic patterns, including where-is,
denotes, let-be, reverse noun phrase, and respectively-list patterns.
This two-step design keeps retrieval flexible while making the final
definition conservative.  If no reliable source span is found, the
definition is left empty rather than guessed.

\paragraph{Relations.}
Relations are built for every ordered equation pair in the same paper.
The direction matters: relation $(A,B)$ asks whether the context around
equation $A$ refers to or depends on equation $B$.  A relation is marked
\textit{strong} when the paper gives direct evidence, such as a citation
with derivation, equivalence, or special-case cues, a plain explicit
citation, or membership in the same detected equation block.  It is
marked \textit{potential} when weaker evidence exists, such as shared
symbols, high \textbf{MathBERT} similarity between equation summaries, or
section proximity.  If no signal fires, the relation is \textit{none}.
Thus strong edges are evidence-backed claims, while potential edges are
graph candidates that may need manual interpretation.

\paragraph{Export.}
Stage~10 finally joins meanings, symbols, definitions, relations, and
audit fields into the required JSON\@.


\section{Conclusion}

\noindent\textbf{Strengths.}

Our approach builds the required dataset by combining structured arXiv
parsing, embedding-based retrieval, deterministic patterns, and audit
validation.  Its main strengths are traceability, graph-ready coverage,
and flexibility across quantum physics writing styles.
The hybrid design also avoids hallucination: \textbf{MathBERT} finds
relevant evidence, but rule-based checks decide what can enter the final
JSON\@.

\noindent\textbf{Weaknesses.}

Some symbol definitions are missing because papers often use notation
without explaining it clearly in words.
Some equation relations are also uncertain: two equations may look
related because they are close together or share symbols, but the paper
may not directly say how they are connected.
The system leaves these cases uncertain instead of inventing an
explanation, because the output must come from the paper.

\noindent\textbf{Possible improvements.}

Future work could fine-tune \textbf{MathBERT} on equation-context pairs
from quantum physics papers, calibrate semantic thresholds for relation
detection, and add more notation-specific definition patterns.  A small
manually checked validation set would also help tune the balance between
coverage and precision.
